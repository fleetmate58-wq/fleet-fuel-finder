  • Calculations follow the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) guidelines for car fringe benefits.
  • The Statutory Method uses a single statutory fraction of 20% applied to the vehicle's base value (post-10 May 2011 contracts).
  • The Operating Cost Method requires a valid 12-week logbook to substantiate business use percentage.
  • FBT is payable by the employer at the rate of 47% on the grossed-up taxable value.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is FBT in Australia?

FBT (the fringe benefit tax) is a tax employers pay on certain non-cash benefits provided to employees or their associates. So what does FBT mean in practice? It means if a business hands over something extra — like a company car, private health cover, or a low-interest loan — the ATO treats it as a fringe benefit and taxes the employer on the value.

The fringe benefits meaning (or fringe benefits definition) under Australian law covers perks provided in respect of employment, in addition to salary or wages. The fringe benefits defined list includes cars, expense payments, housing, entertainment and more. If you've ever wondered what is a fringe benefit in Australia, or what is FBT and what is FBT Australia all about — this is it: a separate tax (FBT AU) run alongside income tax, designed to stop benefits-in-kind avoiding tax.

Who pays fringe benefits tax and how does it work?

Who pays fringe benefits tax? Employers do — not employees. So who pays FBT ultimately comes down to the business providing the benefit, which is one of the biggest surprises for new business owners. The FBT liability sits squarely with the employer, even though what are fringe benefits for employees are enjoyed personally by staff.

How does FBT work? Employers identify every taxable fringe benefit provided during the FBT year, gross it up, apply the 47% rate, and lodge an annual FBT return — that's FBT reporting in a nutshell. What is a taxable fringe benefit? Anything provided in connection with employment that isn't exempt.

Note that payroll tax and FBT are different taxes: payroll tax fringe benefits may be included in state payroll tax calculations (at the grossed-up value) but FBT itself is a separate federal liability.

What are the FBT year dates?

The FBT year in Australia is different from the normal income tax year. FBT year dates run from 1 April to 31 March the following year — so the FBT financial year 2025 covers 1 April 2024 to 31 March 2025.

The FBT year meaning? It's simply the 12-month period the ATO uses to calculate fringe benefits tax. When does FBT year end? 31 March. Employers then have until 21 May (or later if lodging via a tax agent) to lodge their FBT return and pay any liability.

What are some fringe benefits examples and exemptions?

Common fringe benefits examples include: a company car used privately, gym memberships, school fees, low-interest loans, entertainment and meals, car parking, and expense reimbursements. Each type has its own valuation rules.

Not everything attracts tax though. Exempt fringe benefits include a single work-related portable electronic device per year (laptop, phone, tablet), protective clothing, minor benefits under $300, and certain relocation costs. Claiming an FBT exemption correctly can meaningfully reduce an employer's liability — but the rules are strict, and records must be kept.

How is FBT calculated and what is grossing up?

How much is FBT? The FBT rate in Australia is 47%, applied to the grossed-up taxable value — not the raw value of the benefit. Grossing up puts the benefit on equal footing with a salary that would produce the same after-tax amount.

There are two gross-up rates. FBT Type 1 applies where the employer is entitled to claim GST credits on the benefit — the Type 1 rate is 2.0802. The Type 2 gross-up rate of 1.8868 applies where no GST credit is available.

So the formula is: FBT payable = Taxable value × gross-up rate × 47%. Once a benefit is FBT grossed up, the employer multiplies by 47% to find the tax owed. That's why reducing the taxable value — by switching from the statutory method to a logbook — has such a powerful multiplier effect on the final bill.
